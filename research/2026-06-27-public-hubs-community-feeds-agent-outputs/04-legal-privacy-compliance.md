# Legal, Privacy & Compliance

*Raw per-agent output. Domain: legal/privacy/compliance. Archived per `processes/research-workflow.md` §5. Author is not a lawyer; not legal advice. `[needs-counsel]` items require qualified review before build/launch.*

**Bottom line:** Aggregating *public* feeds is not per se illegal and CFAA exposure is low (esp. 9th Cir.), but real risk has migrated to **state contract/tort law (ToS breach, trespass to chattels, misappropriation)** and **copyright in the *selection/arrangement* of compilations**. COPPA is **largely not triggered** by feeds *about* minors under Dayfold's adults-only posture (COPPA keys on collection *from* a child), but it is **not zero-risk**, and a parallel state minors'-privacy patchwork is expanding fast. Republishing creates **DMCA notice-and-takedown obligations** Dayfold does not currently have process for. This feature meaningfully strains Guardrail 4 and modestly strains Guardrail 3.

## 1. Scraping / Aggregation Legality
- **Scraping public (no-login) data likely does NOT violate CFAA.** **Confirmed (9th Cir.; not nationwide).** *hiQ v. LinkedIn* (9th Cir. Apr 2022, post-*Van Buren*): capturing data from publicly accessible pages isn't "access without authorization"; violating a user agreement alone doesn't trigger CFAA. (https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2022/04/18/17-16783.pdf, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HiQ_Labs_v._LinkedIn)
- **The hiQ win did NOT make scraping risk-free; hiQ ultimately lost on state-law grounds.** **Confirmed (settlement, non-precedential).** Dec 2022: $500k judgment + stipulated liability under CA common-law trespass to chattels + misappropriation + injunction. The CFAA "victory" coexisted with tort defeat. Plaintiffs now aim at **contract + tort, not CFAA.** (https://www.proskauer.com/blog/hiq-and-linkedin-reach-proposed-settlement-in-landmark-scraping-case)
- **Logged-off scraping of public data may not breach ToS / public data may be resold.** **Partially confirmed (single district court).** *Meta v. Bright Data* (N.D. Cal. Jan 2024): scraping *while logged off*, the scraper wasn't a bound "user." **Critical limits:** if Dayfold subscribes via an account / accepts ToS / uses an authenticated/API path, the ToS likely binds and this protection evaporates; one district court, not precedent; public-scraping is **not per se legal** (copyright, misappropriation, unjust enrichment, trespass, privacy remain live). (https://newmedialaw.proskauer.com/2024/01/24/california-court-issues-noteworthy-decision-on-breach-of-contract-claims-in-web-scraping-dispute/)

**Net:** `[needs-counsel]` on jurisdiction (Dayfold is multi-state; hiQ is 9th Cir. only). **Posture that fits the case law:** subscribe to feeds *offered for public consumption* (published ICS links, public APIs with permissive terms) rather than scraping behind any login; avoid accepting restrictive ToS; honor robots.txt + rate limits (trespass-to-chattels hygiene).

## 2. Copyright / Database Rights / ToS Exposure
- **Individual facts in listings (date/time/place/team) are not copyrightable.** **Confirmed.** *Feist v. Rural Telephone*, 499 U.S. 340 (1991): facts aren't authored; "sweat of the brow" rejected. (https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/499/340/)
- **A *compilation* CAN be protected — but only original selection/arrangement, not the facts.** **Confirmed.** Compilations get "thin" copyright in selection/coordination/arrangement; never the underlying facts. **US has no separate database right** (unlike EU). **Dayfold risk:** copying raw facts = safe; copying a source's curated layout, descriptive prose, logos, or verbatim listings = infringement risk. **Posture:** ingest *facts*, re-render in Dayfold's own format; do **not** mirror descriptive copy/images/logos verbatim. `[needs-counsel]` on how much descriptive text/branding may carry.
- **ToS breach is viable against ICS/API harvesting even for "public" data.** **Confirmed.** Breach-of-contract is the leading post-CFAA theory; viability turns on contract formation — clickwrap (affirmative agree) strongly enforceable; browsewrap weak. **Dayfold risk:** if Dayfold/operator/agent clicks ToS, creates an account, or registers an API key, it likely forms an enforceable contract + inherits anti-redistribution/no-commercial clauses; **republishing to families is commercial redistribution** commonly prohibited. Most controllable exposure: read each source's terms before ingesting. `[needs-counsel]` per-source.

## 3. Child-Privacy / COPPA (feeds *about* minors)
- **COPPA is triggered by collecting PI *from* a child, not by holding info *about* a child obtained from adults/third parties.** **Confirmed (primary).** 16 CFR §312.2: "Collects... means the gathering of any personal information *from a child*..." Coverage (§312.3): services (a) directed to children <13, or (b) with actual knowledge they collect PI from a child <13. (https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/16/312.2, https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/complying-coppa-frequently-asked-questions)
  - **Application:** Dayfold accounts are adults-only; minors appear only as *subjects* in feed content gathered from public feeds / the parent's own data — **not from the child.** On the face of the Rule, **COPPA's collection trigger is not met**, *provided* Dayfold isn't child-directed and gains no actual knowledge an account holder is <13.
  - **Residual risk (not zero):** `[needs-counsel]` "Directed to children" is a totality test; a "family/kids' events" hub with child-oriented imagery could drift to "mixed audience." Keep marketing/UI **parent/adult-directed.** The **2025 amended COPPA Rule** (eff. June 23 2025; full compliance Apr 22 2026) tightens "mixed audience."
- **A state minors'-privacy patchwork may reach minors' data where COPPA doesn't.** **Confirmed; rapidly evolving.** `[needs-counsel]` AADC laws cover **under-18s** and focus on *service design* "likely to be accessed by children" (high-privacy defaults, minimization, DPIAs, no profiling/geolocation unless necessary). 2025: AR, CT, MT, NE, VT; MD Kids Code (Oct 1 2024); VT AADC (Jan 1 2027); CA CAADCA under 1A challenge. **Why it matters here:** even adults-only, Dayfold would store identifiable minors' data (names, schedules, **recurring precise locations** of games) — a child-safety-sensitive profile broader than COPPA's "from a child" gate. **Get state-law review before ingesting roster/location feeds tied to named minors.** Mitigations: avoid storing minors' surnames + precise recurring locations; treat minor-identifying fields as sensitive; data minimization + retention limits.

## 4. Impact on Dayfold Guardrails 3 & 4
- **Guardrail 3 (restricted-scope/LLM handling):** Community feeds are *public* data → don't trigger the Gmail-restricted-scope/CASA problem (Calendar=sensitive, no CASA; no direct Gmail OAuth at MVP — unchanged). **But** piping feed content (now including identifiable minors' names/schedules/locations) through third-party LLMs to author cards **extends and intensifies** the disclosure requirement: you'd send *children's* PI (third parties who never consented) to an LLM. Recommend explicit privacy-disclosure + LLM data-handling note. ADR-gated. `[needs-counsel]` on whether minor-identifying public data warrants redaction before LLM processing.
- **Guardrail 4 (never system of record; honor delete/export; no spam; no dark patterns):** **Significantly strained — the main governance flag.** Aggregating/republishing schedules makes Dayfold look like the place families *go to see the schedule* — opposite of the guardrail. **Recommend:** every aggregated item **deep-links back to the authoritative source**; UI frames feeds as a *mirror/pointer*, never canonical. **Delete/export:** republished third-party content complicates "honor delete" — Dayfold would hold data about third parties (other families' children in a roster) with no account + no delete pathway — a genuine new obligation gap. `[needs-counsel]` **No spam:** if feed cards generate notifications, volume control matters. **Verdict: ADR-class** (scope + customer-data handling + maintenance burden); Proposed ADR + operator sign-off before build.

## 5. DMCA / Takedown & Attribution
- **An aggregator republishing third-party content incurs DMCA notice-and-takedown obligations + should rely on §512 safe harbor.** **Confirmed (with eligibility caveat).** §512 shields providers from monetary liability for third-party material *if*: register a designated agent + publish contact; repeat-infringer termination policy; accommodate standard technical measures; expeditiously remove on valid notice/red-flag knowledge. (https://www.copyright.gov/512/, https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF11478)
  - **Eligibility caveat / `[needs-counsel]`:** §512(c) is for material stored *at the direction of users*. An aggregator that **affirmatively selects + republishes** feeds may be the *content provider*, not a passive host — **weakening/forfeiting safe-harbor** + exposing direct/secondary infringement for any non-fact content copied. Makes §2's "render facts in your own format, don't mirror prose/images" rule load-bearing.
  - **Attribution:** no general "attribution cures infringement" defense, **but** source-linking is advisable: supports Guardrail-4 "not system of record" framing; reduces misappropriation/false-endorsement risk; aligns with feed terms permitting republication only with attribution. **Operational requirement if it ships:** register a DMCA designated agent + takedown process — Dayfold has neither today.
